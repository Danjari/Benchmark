## Feature 1: Voice-Based Learning

### NEW 1.1 — Wollny et al. (2021) [ No existent ]
**Citation:** Wollny, S., Schneider, J., Di Mitri, D., Weidlich, J., Rittberger, M., & Drachsler, H. (2021). Are we there yet? A systematic literature review on chatbots in education. *Frontiers in Artificial Intelligence*, 4, 654524.
**Link:** https://doi.org/10.3389/frai.2021.654524
**Role:** 2021 systematic review of conversational AI (voice-capable agents) in education. Covers engagement, learning outcomes, and design patterns across 74 studies. Establishes that AI-driven conversational tutoring is empirically supported, not a novelty. Anchor citation for the voice feature.

---

### NEW 1.2 — Mayer (2021)
**Citation:** Mayer, R. E. (2021). Evidence-based principles for how to design effective instructional videos. *Journal of Applied Research in Memory and Cognition*, 10(2), 229–240.
**Link:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jarmac.2021.03.007
**Role:** Mayer's most recent synthesis confirming that spoken narration outperforms on-screen text (modality principle) and that interactive voice delivery improves learning over passive reading. Core scientific backing for the voice feature — 2021, not 2003.

---

### NEW 1.3 — Fiorella & Mayer (2016)
**Citation:** Fiorella, L., & Mayer, R. E. (2016). Eight ways to promote generative learning. *Educational Psychology Review*, 28(4), 717–741.
**Link:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10648-015-9348-9
**Role:** Establishes the generative processing framework: engaging multiple sensory channels (verbal + auditory) leads to deeper encoding and better transfer. Explains WHY the voice feature produces learning gains, not just that it does. More recent and broader than dual-coding framing from the 1990s.

## Feature 2: Visual / Drawing-Based Learning

### NEW 2.1 — Rau (2017)
**Citation:** Rau, M. A. (2017). Conditions for the effectiveness of multiple visual representations in enhancing STEM learning. *Educational Psychology Review*, 29(4), 717–761.
**Link:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s10648-016-9365-3
**Role:** 2017 comprehensive review of when and why visual representations (diagrams, animations) improve learning. Identifies the conditions that make AI-generated visual explanations effective: concept complexity, spatial nature, and student prior knowledge. Primary citation for the drawing feature.

---

### NEW 2.2 — Walkington & Bernacki (2019)
**Citation:** Walkington, C., & Bernacki, M. L. (2019). Personalizing algebra to students' individual interests in an intelligent tutoring system: Moderators of impact. *International Journal of Artificial Intelligence in Education*, 29(1), 58–88.
**Link:** https://doi.org/10.1007/s40593-018-0168-1
**Role:** 2019 ITS paper on personalizing content delivery to student-expressed preferences. Justifies the adaptive modality logic: when a student's onboarding survey indicates a preference for visual explanations, the system honors that preference. Frames personalization as preference-based (defensible) rather than fixed "visual learner type" (contested).

## Feature 3: Mastery-Based Assessment Gating

### NEW 3.1 — VanLehn (2011)
**Citation:** VanLehn, K. (2011). The relative effectiveness of human tutoring, intelligent tutoring systems, and other tutoring systems. *Educational Psychologist*, 46(4), 197–221.
**Link:** https://doi.org/10.1080/00461520.2011.611369
**Role:** Meta-analysis showing ITS achieves 0.76 SD learning gains over conventional instruction. Connects the mastery-gating design to the ITS literature — establishes the empirical basis for AI-driven mastery assessment in educational technology.

---

### NEW 3.2 — Kulik & Fletcher (2016)
**Citation:** Kulik, J. A., & Fletcher, J. D. (2016). Effectiveness of intelligent tutoring systems: A meta-analytic review. *Review of Educational Research*, 86(1), 42–78.
**Link:** https://doi.org/10.3102/0034654315581420
**Role:** 2016 meta-analysis of 50 ITS studies confirming consistent positive effects on learning. The most recent large-scale evidence base for AI-driven formative assessment and mastery tracking. Pairs with VanLehn (2011) for a decade-spanning quantitative case.

---

### NEW 3.3 — Shute (2008)
**Citation:** Shute, V. J. (2008). Focus on formative feedback. *Review of Educational Research*, 78(1), 153–189.
**Link:** https://doi.org/10.3102/0034654307313795
**Role:** Comprehensive review of formative feedback design in learning systems. Establishes what makes feedback effective after assessment — not just whether to give feedback, but when, how, and at what grain size. Directly applicable to the in-chat quiz gating: the quiz unlocks the next node AND provides the student with actionable feedback on where their understanding stands.

## Carried Over (Foundational — Do Not Replace)

### KEEP — Bloom (1984)
**Citation:** Bloom, B. S. (1984). The 2 sigma problem: The search for methods of group instruction as effective as one-to-one tutoring. *Educational Researcher*, 13(6), 4–16.
**Link:** https://doi.org/10.3102/0013189X013006004
**Role:** Non-negotiable. THE citation for mastery-based learning. Reviewers expect to see it. No modern paper replaces this empirical result.

### KEEP — Ainsworth (2006)
**Citation:** Ainsworth, S. (2006). DeFT: A conceptual framework for considering learning with multiple representations. *Learning and Instruction*, 16(3), 183–198.
**Link:** https://doi.org/10.1016/j.learninstruc.2006.03.001
**Role:** Landmark framework paper still heavily cited. Explains when combining visual and textual representations helps (and when it does not) — justifies the AI's adaptive decision to use drawing for spatial concepts and text for abstract reasoning.
